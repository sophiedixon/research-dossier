INTRODUCTION

‘Space defines landscape whereas space combined with memory defines place’ Lucy Lippard

Place, commonly perceived as something we inhabit, is also something we experience. As Lucy Lippard describes is, place is ‘something seen from the inside’. As a creator, I am fascinated with how places are experienced, both by myself and by others.

To be a recollection, rather than a fantasy, the memory of a place must be rooted in facts, and artefacts. But what happens to our memories of the past when the physical places they reference threaten to disappear? What happens when multiple stories of the past reside in the same place? Which stories of the past can we believe?

The context for this experiment is 'Scholz's House', a ruin in a remote village of Srbská in today's Czech Republic.

SCHOLZ'S HOUSE

Ruins stand as reminders, memory is always incomplete, always imperfect, always falling into ruin’ Rebecca Solnit

Each year a group of elderly Germans make a pilgrimage to the village of Srbská, where together they create out of the landscape their own imagined village - Wünschendorf - from which they and their families were forcibly removed in 1946 together with three million other 'Sudeten', ethnic German, citizens of the then Czechoslovakia.

Each year since 2009 I have filmed and interviewed the group, one of whom is Mr Scholz, who left House Number 51, which I call 'Scholtz's House' as a child. Working with Mr Scholz, and Mrs Jirakova, the daughter of the Czech family who moved into the repossessed house, I have explored and uncovered events and memories bound to Number 51.

This process of discovery has given rise to my own unique and constantly changing narrative of the lives and events this house has witnessed and made me question how we can be sure of any one fixed story of the past
